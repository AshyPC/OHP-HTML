Rej 380: Marvellous Grace of Our

Loving Lord

1. Marvellous grace of our loving Lord, grace that exceeds our sin and our guilt! Yonder on Calvary's mount out-poured, there where the blood of the Lamb was spilt.
Grace, grace, God's grace, grace that will pardon and cleanse within; grace, grace, God's grace, grace that is greater than all our sin!
2. Sin and despair, like the seawaves cold, threaten the soul with infinite loss; grace that is greater, yes, grace untold, points to the refuge, the mighty cross.
Grace, grace, God's grace, grace that will pardon and cleanse within; grace, grace, God's grace, grace that is greater than all our sin!
3. Dark is the stain that we cannot hide; what can avail to wash it away? Look: there is flowing a crimson tide -- whiter than snow you may be today.
Grace, grace, God's grace, grace that will pardon and cleanse within; grace, grace, God's grace, grace that is greater than all our sin!
4. Marvellous, infinite, matchless grace, freely bestowed on all who believe! You that are longing to see His face, will you this moment His grace receive?

PP 6: From the Squalor

1. From the squalor of a borrowed stable, by the Spirit and a virgin's faith, to the anguish and the pain of scandal, came the Saviour of the human race!
But the skies were filled with the praise of heaven, shepherds listen as the angels tell of the gift of God come down to man at the dawning of Immanuel.
2. King of heaven now the Friend of sinners, Humble servant in the Father's hands, Filled with power and the Holy Spirit, Filled with mercy for the broken man.
Yes, He walked my road and He felt my pain, Joys and sorrows that I know so well; Yes His righteous steps give me hope again - I will follow my Immanuel.

Job 1 page 374
There are two ways to view suffering: one is from a Christian point of view and the other is the non-Christian point of view.
As we look at Job’s story, the question can be raised if this book is reliable because of questions on historical facts in the story. If this man Job did not really exist and this story is fiction, I believe that it cannot be suitable to speak about suffering.
Suffering not always determined by sin
There are times where death and misfortune do occur for the sins of an individual. 1. Eli (1 Samuel 2)
2. David (his son Absalom)
In modern times, there are some speakers around the world who directly relate natural disaster as God’s punishment for our sins
Not the case with Job. Job is complimented by God in two aspects of his character
Job 1:1 “In the land of Uz there lived a man whose name was Job. This man was blameless and upright; he feared God and shunned evil.”
Job 1:1 “In the land of Uz there lived a man whose name was Job. This man was blameless and upright; he feared God and shunned evil.”
Job didn’t touch anything that would displease God
Job was wealthy
Job 2-3 “He had seven sons and three daughters, and he owned seven thousand sheep, three thousand camels, five hundred yoke of oxen and five hundred donkeys, and had a large number of servants. He was the greatest man among all the people of the East.”
Job 1:5 “When a period of feasting had run its course, Job would make arrangements for them to be purified. Early in the morning he would sacrifice a burnt offering for each of them, thinking, “Perhaps my children have sinned and cursed God in their hearts.” This was Job’s regular custom.”
God’s confidence in Job
Job 1:8 “Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil.”
The Satan is actually questioning whether Job’s faith is legitimate
Job 1:11 “But now stretch out your hand and strike everything he has, and he will surely curse you to your face.”
For most religious people, faith is about what you can get out of it, rather than sincere love and devotion to God, and knowing who he is and loving him for who he is.
Job 1:12 “Very well, then, everything he has is in your power, but on the man himself do not lay a finger.”
God is very much in control of everything.
The disasters that struck Job’s possessions and family were of earthly and heavenly nature. Earthly - Sabeans took the oxen and donkeys, and killed the servants(v. 15); Chaldeans took camels and killed the servants (v. 17) Heavenly - Lightning struck sheep and servants (v. 16); whirlwind killed Job’s children (v. 19)
Why did Job suffer? John 9:1-5 “As he went along, he saw a man blind from birth. His disciples asked him, “Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?” “Neither this man nor his parents sinned,” said Jesus, “but this happened so that the works of God might be displayed in him. As long as it is day, we must do the works of him who sent me. Night is coming, when no one can work. While I am in the world, I am the light of the world.”
God gives and takes away
Job is said to have “fell to the ground in worship.”
He showed full reverence and respect to God.
Job 1:20 “At this, Job got up and tore his robe and shaved his head”
What Job remembered is where he came from
Job 1:21 “Naked I came from my mother’s womb, and naked I will depart.”
When we are born we are introduced to the world with nothing
God’s creation of human beings.
Gen 2:7 “Then the Lord God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being.”
Job is recognising the puniness of us as humans, and the greatness of God.
Job 1:21 “The Lord gave and the Lord has taken away; may the name of the Lord be praised.” It all belongs to God
No Blaming
The words “curse” and “blame” is used in matters directly related to God.
“he will surely curse you to your face.” (1:11; 2:5)
“Job did not sin by charging God with wrongdoing.” (1:22) “His wife said to him, “Are you still maintaining your integrity? Curse God and die!” He replied, “You are talking like a foolish[b] woman. Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?”” (Job 2:9-10)
It is important to understand that blaming God is a sin itself.
There is nothing greater than having a relationship with Christ
“But whatever were gains to me I now consider loss for the sake of Christ. What is more, I consider everything a loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord, for whose sake I have lost all things. I consider them garbage, that I may gain Christ and be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which is through faith in Christ.” (Phil. 3:7-9)
